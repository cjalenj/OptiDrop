The Problem

Roughly 80 million people around the world, including 3 million Americans, are currently diagnosed with glaucoma, the leading cause of irreversible blindness. (Davuluru et. al) The primary treatment of glaucoma involves the daily application of eye drops to reduce intraocular pressure and prevent the progression of the disease. However, the process of self-administering eye drops presents a significant challenge for many patients, particularly the elderly, due to issues such as difficulty in aiming the drops and dispensing the accurate amount of drops due to bottle variance. This often leads to improper dosage, patient frustration, and waste of medication and money. Therefore, the objective of this project is to design and engineer an automated eye drop dispenser device to assist with the application of eye drops. This solution aims to simplify the eye drop application process, ensuring that patients can administer their medication independently, accurately, and efficiently. The ultimate objective is to enhance the quality of life for glaucoma patients, ease the treatment process, and potentially reduce the waste of eye drops and money.

The Stats

Interviews with 4 ophthalmologists and 25 glaucoma patients aged 60-80 revealed key challenges in eye drop application.

60% reported difficulty in aiming drops accurately

20% struggled with tilting their heads back

Due to variances between bottle designs, 80% noted inconsistencies in pressure required per drop with the majority saying that some bottles accidentally dispense more drops.

From additional research

From a study conducted by Els Mehuys et al. that interviewed 678 patients with an average age of 68.9 years about their experience with eye drops.

3% of the participants were able to accurately administer their eye drops

18.3% struggled to get a drop in their eye

14.6% struggled with dispensing too many drops

12.2% found the bottle hard to squeeze

From a study conducted by Dr. Tony Tsai et al. that interviewed 253 glaucoma patients with a mean age of 71.5 years about their experience with eye drops.

82.6% of the participants self-administered their drops, while 17.4% relied on the assistance due to reasons like poor vision, dexterity issues, and difficulty dispensing a single drop
